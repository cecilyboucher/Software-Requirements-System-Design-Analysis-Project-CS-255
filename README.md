1.) Briefly summarize the DriverPass project. Who was the client? What type of system did they want you to design?
Liam, the owner of DriverPass, and his IT officer, Ian, needed a system to improve driver training services for students preparing for their driving tests at the DMV. The system had to include online classes, practice tests, on-the-road training sessions, and robust data management accessible both online and offline. They also required features for user account management, security roles, change tracking, and detailed reporting. The main goal was to ensure smooth booking, scheduling, and management of driving lessons for different user roles such as the owner, IT officer, secretary, and customers.
2.) What did you do particularly well?
I was good at breaking down complex requirements into clear, actionable steps. I focused on making the information easy to understand and follow, whether it was creating UML diagrams or interpreting statistical results. This approach helped structure the project effectively and ensured that all necessary details were covered comprehensively.

4.) How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your system design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?
Reading the DriverPass interview, I paid attention to Liam's vision and requirements to understand what users need for the DriverPass system. I identified important features such as online classes, practice tests, on-the-road training, and different user roles. Based on this, I designed the system to ensure secure data access, robust reservation tracking, and clear reporting for specific user roles like the owner, IT officer, secretary, and customers. Designing with user needs in mind is vital to make the system practical, efficient, and user-friendly. A design that meets user requirements enhances satisfaction, maximizes usability, and ensures the system solves real user problems, leading to successful adoption and usage. Prioritizing user needs makes the design a tailored solution, fostering better engagement and trust rather than a generic one.
5.) How do you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a system?
Creating software is like putting together a puzzle, blending art and science. It all starts with understanding the user's needs and requirements by talking to them, using surveys, and watching them. Then, I write detailed descriptions of how the system should work, making sure to include all user interactions and processes. After that, I create diagrams to show how the system will be built. When it's time to build the software, I divide the work into smaller tasks and improve the product based on user feedback through a series of development and testing phases.
